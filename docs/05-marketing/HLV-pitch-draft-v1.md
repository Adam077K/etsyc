## SLIDE 3 — The problem
**On screen (74pt):**
> There's a person behind
> everything you buy.
> You just never meet them.

**Spoken:**
There's a person behind everything you buy. A story, a craft, a reason they do this — the kind of thing you'd love to hear, if you ever got the chance. But you don't. All you see is a photo, a price, a rating. And now — any of it can be faked. The photo, the reviews, even the face. So we scroll, we buy, and we feel nothing, from no one. But it wasn't always like this: walk into a real store, and you *meet* the person who made it. They tell you who it's for, the story, the little things — and that's why you trust them. We just never brought that online. A maker is an *artist*, not a marketer or a web designer — so the human disappears, on *both* sides.

## SLIDE 4 — Introducing
**On screen:**

> *(Hebrew for "voice.")*

**Spoken:** So we built it. We call it **Kol** — in Hebrew, that means *voice* — because the voices of the people behind these products deserve to be heard.

## SLIDE 6 — It works like this
**On screen (bullets):**
> · A maker talks — a few minutes. Their voice, their hands.
> · The tech brings their world to life — their style, their beliefs.
> · You scroll real people making real things.
> · Tap in — their voice plays on, in a store that feels like them.
> · Every word is theirs — tap it, hear them say it.
> · You don't compare. You meet them. You buy from them.

**Spoken (the scene — the demo, in words):**
Here's what it feels like. You open it, and there's no grid — just people, real hands making real things, their voice telling you why. One stops you: a woman in her studio, talking about the day she started. You tap in, and her voice keeps going while her whole world opens around you — her colors, her workshop, the thing she made, and why. She never had to be a designer; she just *talked*, and the technology brought her world to life — the way she'd show you if you walked into her shop. And the part you can't fake? Every word in her store is her *own voice* — tap any line, and you hear her say it. You didn't compare her to anyone. You met her. And you bought from her.

## SLIDE 7 — This helps buyers, makers, and Etsy by
**On screen (3 bullets):**
> · Buyers see behind the product — the hands, the human — and want to support.
> · Makers who were never designers finally show their creativity, their beliefs — alive.
> · The promise comes true: real people, made human again.

**Spoken:**
That's better for everyone. The buyer sees *behind* the product — the hands, the person — and wants to support them. The maker, who was never a designer, finally shows their creativity and their beliefs, brought to life. They stay the artist; we just give them a way to be heard. Because in a world full of fakes, a real human is the one thing you trust — and when you trust someone, you want to support them. Even pay a little more, if it comes to that.

### How this draft answers the board's three knives
- **Trust self-own → fixed:** trust = her own voice, cited to timecode ("tap it, hear her say it"). No "AI bounded" claim; AI is invisible.
- **Seller apathy → answered on stage:** "a few minutes," "she never had to be a designer, she just talked," "the store builds itself around her voice."
- **Talking-heads UX → fixed:** the feed leads with the *making* (hands/material/time), voice on top.
- **Team lanes:** closing line names three build lanes to recruit into.
- **Mutual enablement (founder):** the maker is *empowered* to express creativity/beliefs via the tech — the engine of the buyer's "see behind the product."
- **Premium (your call):** "support" leads; "even pay a little more, if it comes to that" is the honest tail.
- **Name → mission:** Kol = voice; "the voices of small businesses deserve to be heard." The real-store analogy is the bridge (you hear the owner in real life; Kol brings that online). Vision echoes the sponsor's own challenge line ("connect with the real people behind the things they love").
