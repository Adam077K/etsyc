# Survey — "Can buyers tell what's AI-made?" (paste-ready for Google Forms)

**Thesis under test:** Buyers cannot reliably tell when a product is AI-made, and when they
find out, their trust drops. (Etsy × Columbia challenge · buyer knowledge-gap cell.)

**Target respondent:** online shoppers only (hard screener exits non-shoppers).
**Length:** ~12 quick taps + 1 optional text field — under 2 minutes.
**Time to build in Forms:** ~10 min (mostly uploading your 4 images).

   "Edit after submit".)

## SECTION 1 — Screener  *(this is its own section)*

**Q1.** How often do you buy physical products online (clothes, home goods, gifts, handmade items, etc.)?
- Type: Multiple choice · **Required**
- Options: `Never` · `A few times a year` · `About once a month` · `Weekly or more`
- **Branch logic:** "Go to section based on answer" → if **Never**, jump to the final
  **Thank-you section** (they exit). All others → continue to Section 2.

---

## SECTION 2 — Baseline attitudes

**Q2.** When shopping online, how much do you care whether a product is made by a real person
vs. mass-produced or AI-made?
- Type: Linear scale 1–5 · Required · Labels: 1 = `Don't care at all`, 5 = `Care a lot`

**Q3.** Before today, did you know sellers can use AI to create product *images*, *descriptions*,
or even the *products themselves*?
- Type: Multiple choice · Required
- Options: `Yes — well aware` · `Vaguely aware` · `No — didn't know`

---

## SECTION 3 — Your confidence  *(ask this BEFORE the test — it's the money variable)*

**Q4.** How good do you think you are at telling whether a product was made/generated by AI
vs. a real person?
- Type: Linear scale 1–5 · Required · Labels: 1 = `Not good at all`, 5 = `Very good`

> 📊 This is the single most important comparison in the study: **claimed confidence (Q4) vs.
> actual score (Section 4).** If people rate themselves 4–5 but score ~50%, you've proven the
> knowledge gap cold.

## How to read the results (what proves the thesis)

| Finding | Comparison | What confirms the thesis |
|---|---|---|
| **The core proof** | Detection score (count correct of Q5–Q8) | Average near **50% = chance** → buyers genuinely can't tell. |
| **Confidence gap** (the killer chart) | Q4 confidence vs. actual score | High confidence + low score = they *think* they can tell but can't. |
| **Does it matter** | Q9 | Most say "less likely to buy" → the misperception has real cost. |
| **Transparency demand** | Q10 + Q11 | Majority want disclosure → validates the "knowledge/transparency gap." |
| **Awareness baseline** | Q3 | Many "didn't know" → the knowledge gap exists even before the test. |
| **Segment** | Q2, Q12, Q13 | Do people who "care" (Q2) score any better? Usually not — a strong slide. |

**One-line result you're hoping to report:** *"Respondents rated their own AI-spotting ability
at X/5 but scored only Y% correct — and Z% said they'd be less likely to buy once they knew."*
