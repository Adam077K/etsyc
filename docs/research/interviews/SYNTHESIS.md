# Discovery Synthesis — Etsy × Columbia Challenge

**Corpus:** 4 buyer street interviews (files 01–04, English, near Columbia/NYC) + 1 expert-seller phone call (file 05, Hebrew Etsy seller, translated).
**Sample:** n = 4 buyers + 1 seller. **Directional, not conclusive.** Every supply-side claim rests on a single voice (see §7).
**Data caveat:** File 05's sharpest 54s (the small-business-vs-price answer) was silently truncated by the primary transcription model and recovered only from a second model — it is single-sourced and unconfirmed against audio (README §"READ THIS FIRST"). It is also the most valuable material in the corpus, so it is flagged wherever quoted.
**Method caveat:** The buyer interviewers ask **leading questions** ("would you feel upset? indifferent?"; "unique pieces that doesn't exist in Amazon... more convincing to buy?"). Buyer enthusiasm for authenticity is real but almost certainly **inflated by the framing**. Treat direction as solid, magnitude as soft.

---

## 1. TL;DR — the 5 things that matter

1. **Buyers punish "fake" hard and uniformly.** All 4 buyers react to fake / AI-made / drop-shipped goods with the same emotional arc: betrayal → distrust → defection ("wouldn't buy from there again"). The strongest, most consistent signal in the corpus. But it was partly led by the questions.
2. **"Authenticity" is really "not a scam."** Buyers don't want handmade for craft's sake — they want proof the thing is *real* and *as advertised*. Julia's whole reason to buy: the stone "was real," not plastic faking volcanic rock (01). The pain is being *scammed*, not missing artisanship.
3. **Stated preference >> actual behavior.** Buyers praise local/unique, yet one literally cannot recall ever buying from a non-commercialized store (02) and another, 5+ years in NYC, barely has (04). High professed value, low frequency. This gap is the most important finding for a business.
4. **Price beats principle — on both sides.** The seller *prefers* small businesses "but sometimes the cost is expensive, so I'll go to the big business" (05). A buyer *canceled an in-store visit after comparison-shopping online* on price (04). Everyone says local; everyone defects on price.
5. **Buyers and sellers meet through totally different doors.** The seller gets 100% of customers from **platform search** ("I show up in the search pages... by chance he lands on me," 05). Buyers discover local through **serendipity, physical markets, and advertising buzz** — none described searching a platform for "local/handmade." Supply-side is algorithmic; demand-side is accidental. That mismatch is the whitespace.

## 2. Buyer themes (files 01–04)

### Theme A — "Real / as-advertised" is the core buying trigger (4/4)
Not craft, not ethics — **authenticity as anti-scam insurance**. Uniqueness and ingredient transparency are the proof-points buyers cite.
- 01 (Julia): *"it was real. 'Cause oftentimes... they're just kind of like plastic made to look like volcanic rock, but that one was real."*
- 02: *"maybe something unique to that shop I would be looking for"* + *"evidence of good quality."*
- 04: *"they all had their proper ingredient labels at the back, and if you read it, it was nothing, nothing fake."*
Support: strong (4/4). Caveat: 02 and 04 were somewhat led toward "unique vs. Amazon."

### Theme B — Fake / AI / drop-shipped triggers betrayal → distrust → defection (4/4)
The single most uniform pattern. Same shape every time.
- 01: *"I'd feel upset"* → later *"'Cause I got scammed"* → *"I just wouldn't buy from there again."*
- 02: *"I'd feel slightly cheated... it would take away from the feeling of uniqueness"* → *"I wouldn't trust."*
- 03: *"pretty disappointed 'cause it's... different to what they were trying to advertise"* → *"I would lose interest."*
- 04: on lower-quality-than-claimed ingredients — *"yes"* (would feel distrust; corrected from the file's worst-confidence token, README).
Support: strong (4/4). Caveat: heavily led ("would you feel upset? indifferent?"). Note buyers bundle "AI-made" *with* "fake/drop-shipped/low-quality" — we cannot tell if "AI" means anything distinct to them or is just a synonym for "not genuine."

### Theme C — What stops them: it's not top of mind, and price wins (mixed)
Local/handmade is aspirational, not habitual.
- 02: *"I can't r- really remember any— ... Nothing within recent memory, no."* (last non-commercialized purchase)
- 04: *"I haven't been to a lot of farmer's market over here. I don't think there's a lot in New York"* — despite *"over five years now."*
- 04 (price beats the in-store premium): *"I was thinking to stop by the store... then I checked the prices and I canceled."*
Support: moderate. The friction is **low salience + availability perception + price**, not a stated objection to local itself.

### Theme D — Mass-produced vs. AI-made: fine until it's *disguised* (4/4)
Buyers don't rail against mass production per se — they rail against **deception**. A cheap perfume is great *if* the label is honest (04). The offense is a premium/"boutique" thing secretly being drop-shipped (01's $50 Anthropologie cup scenario). AI/fake is judged as a *trust violation*, not a *manufacturing method*.
Support: strong but interviewer-scaffolded.

### Theme E — Discovery is serendipity, physical, and buzz — not search (4/4)
- 01: the seller himself — *"the guy there, he was really nice"* (in-person rapport).
- 03: advertising buzz — *"everybody's, like, advertising, like, tacos. So... it makes me very interested."*
- 04: physically stumbling on street markets — *"weekend markets closer to the 59th Columbus Circle"*; and comparison-shopping online *before* deciding.
No buyer described going to a platform and searching for "local" or "handmade." Demand-side discovery is **accidental and offline**.

## 3. The seller reality (file 05) — what buyers can't tell you

An actual Etsy seller (costumes, seasonal — Halloween). This is the supply side, and it contradicts the romantic framing of "independent commerce." **All of §3 is n = 1.**

- **Discovery = 100% platform search, zero relationship.** *"The truth is I have almost no interaction at all with the customer... it's a huge platform, and I show up in the search pages. So let's say if someone searches for a swan costume and by chance he lands on me, then he reaches out."* The buyer's warm "the guy was really nice" (01) has **no counterpart on the seller side** — she never meets them.
- **Zero marketing effort by design.** *"I don't do any marketing at all. Sometimes I do... paid promotion — really, really in a small way... I don't pull customers to me, not through social media."*
- **Seasonal side-hustle, low investment.** *"it's not my main business, so I... don't really invest much in it"*; *"whatever comes out of it, I'm happy with it."*
- **Volume: ~100 sales in a good season.** *"the year before... in my season I had a hundred sales, which isn't bad."*
- **Blames tax, not the platform, for a down year.** *"Last year I had a relatively weak year because they'd just raised the taxes."* Etsy is viewed as a neutral, reliable traffic faucet — *"there's very nice traffic there."*
- **She is not an online shopper herself.** *"I'm not much of an online customer."* The person selling online is not the person the buyer interviews describe.
- **THE money quote (recovered tail, single-sourced):** *"By and large I prefer from a small business, but sometimes the cost is expensive, so I'll go to the big business."* → *"But I prefer small businesses."* A self-identified small-business advocate who defects to big business on price — in the same breath.

## 4. Tensions & contradictions (the most valuable findings)

1. **Say-local / defect-on-price (headline).** Seller: prefers small businesses *and* leaves them for cost, back-to-back (05). Buyer echo: 04 cancels the store trip on price. **Stated values do not survive contact with a price tag.** This is the finding to build strategy around.
2. **Professed authenticity-love vs. near-zero local purchase frequency.** 4/4 buyers exalt "real/unique," yet 02 recalls *no* recent local purchase and 04 (5+ yrs NYC) barely any. The market *admires* local more than it *buys* it.
3. **"Online can't match the store" vs. buying online anyway.** 04: *"No, absolutely not"* (online can't replicate the market experience) — then chooses online-informed price over the in-store experience minutes later.
4. **Warm buyer story vs. cold seller reality.** Buyers frame local commerce as human ("the guy was really nice," 01). The seller's actual experience is *"almost no interaction at all with the customer"* (05). The human connection buyers say they value is, on Etsy, **absent by design**.
5. **Discovery mismatch.** Sellers exist only through search ranking; buyers find local only by accident/offline. The two sides are optimized for different doors and rarely meet through the same one.

## 5. Jobs-to-be-done

- **Buyer hires local/handmade to:** *avoid being scammed and own something genuine.* Functional job: get a real, as-advertised, ideally unique item (proof = honest labels, visible quality, no hidden drop-ship). Emotional job: feel smart and un-cheated, not "artisan-virtuous." Price is a hard constraint, not a value they'll pay unlimited premium for.
- **Seller hires Etsy to:** *earn passive, low-effort seasonal income with no marketing burden.* Etsy is a search-traffic faucet, not a brand or relationship channel — *"whatever comes out of it, I'm happy."* She is *not* hiring it to build customer relationships, and *not* investing to grow it.

These two JTBD barely overlap: buyers want a human authenticity story; the seller wants a hands-off transaction engine.

---

## 6. Open questions & who to probe next

1. **Interview a committed local shopper.** Every buyer here was low-frequency. We have no voice of someone who *actually and regularly* buys local — the person our whole thesis assumes exists. **Highest-priority gap.**
2. **Interview a full-time, livelihood-dependent Etsy seller.** File 05 is a seasonal side-hustler with "don't invest much" incentives. A maker whose rent depends on Etsy will have opposite views on marketing, price defection, and platform dependence.
3. **Quantify the price premium.** Nobody named a number. How much more will a buyer pay for "real"? What is "expensive" to the seller? Willingness-to-pay is the missing variable behind every tension in §4.
4. **Does "AI-made" mean anything specific to buyers?** Interviewers fused fake/AI/drop-shipped into one prompt. Ask about AI-made goods *in isolation* to see whether it is a distinct concern or just a synonym for "fake."
5. **How would a buyer find a local seller *online*?** No buyer described platform search for local. Probe whether an authenticity/anti-dupe signal at point-of-search would change behavior — that is the potential product.
6. **Re-run with neutral, non-leading questions** to de-bias Themes A/B/D before trusting their magnitude.

## 7. Sample-size honesty

- **n = 4 buyers + 1 seller, all NYC/Columbia.** Directional only. Do not present any of this as validated.
- **Everything in §3 (seller reality) is a single voice** — including "100 sales/season," "blames tax," and the price-defection quote.
- The **price-defection headline** is 1 seller quote + 1 partial buyer echo (04). Strong as a hypothesis, weak as a fact.
- Themes A, B, D are 4/4 *but interviewer-led* — high consistency, inflated magnitude.
- The corpus's best quote (05, 03:21) is **single-sourced from the recovered transcript tail** and never checked against audio. Verify before external use.
- **No repeat/high-frequency local buyer, no full-time seller, no non-NYC voice** yet. These are the three biggest blind spots.
