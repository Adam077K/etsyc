## 2. What we actually learned (the 6 that matter)

1. **The buyer's real job is "don't get scammed," not "support artisans."** Authenticity = anti-scam
   insurance. Julia bought because the stone *"was real,"* not out of craft romance. Build for
   *proof of realness*, not for artisan virtue.

2. **Say/do gap is the headline finding.** 4/4 buyers exalt local/unique; one recalls **no** recent
   local purchase, another (5+ yrs NYC) barely any. High professed value, low frequency. **A business
   cannot be built on what people admire — only on what they do.**

3. **Price beats principle — on both sides, back-to-back.** The seller: *"I prefer small business, but
   sometimes the cost is expensive, so I'll go to the big business."* A buyer canceled an in-store trip
   after price-checking online. Values do not survive contact with a price tag.

4. **The human connection buyers say they value is absent by design on Etsy.** Buyers frame local as
   human (*"the guy was really nice"*); the seller's lived reality is *"almost no interaction at all with
   the customer."* Discovery happens; **relationship does not.** This is the whitespace, and it matches
   the Day-3 reframe exactly: *discovery that becomes relationship.*

5. **The seller is the higher-pain, lower-alternative user.** Buyers who hit a fake just walk away —
   low pain, many options. The seller has no tools, no relationship, no marketing bandwidth, and is
   overloaded (file 06: *"shouldn't have to be a CMO at the same time as a CFO... a CX officer"*).
   **If we wedge on one side first, the evidence points to the seller.**

6. **"AI-made" is real but undifferentiated.** Buyers bundle AI/fake/drop-shipped/low-quality into one
   "not genuine" blob. We cannot yet claim "AI" is a distinct concern. Needs isolation before we build
   an "AI-detection" story into the pitch.

## 3. What to take forward — the direction this points to

**One trust gap, two ends.** The buyer end ("is this real?") and the seller end ("how do I prove I'm
real without becoming a full marketing department?") are the **same gap** seen from both sides.

- **Day-3 reframe (buyer end):** move the buyer↔maker *relationship* on-platform — "discovery that
  becomes relationship." Etsy has discovery, no relationship.
- **File-06 thesis (seller end):** give the overloaded solo seller **trust/transparency tooling** —
  prove authenticity, hit a quality standard, control what the buyer sees, *"take the slack for the
  seller."*

**These are complementary, not competing.** Seller-side transparency tooling is *what produces the
buyer-visible trust signal* that lets discovery turn into relationship. The strongest venture shape on
the current evidence:

> **Seller-first trust tooling that emits a buyer-visible "this is real / this is the human" signal.**
> It has a desperate user (the seller, file 05/06), it serves the sponsor directly (Etsy *Buyer*
> Experience — the buyer sees trust), and it attacks the say/do gap by lowering the buyer's scam-risk at
> the exact moment of discovery.

This is a **candidate**, not a decision. Day 4 (solutioning) is where the wedge gets chosen. §5 is what
has to be true for this to survive.

## 4. The tensions to resolve before Day 4

1. **Which end do we wedge on?** Seller-first (higher pain, direct sponsor value) vs. buyer-first
   (larger market, but low-pain and led responses). Evidence leans seller-first; magnitude is soft.
2. **Is there willingness to pay?** Nobody named a number — not the buyer's premium for "real," not the
   seller's budget. This is the single missing variable behind every tension.
3. **Does buyer behavior actually change with a trust signal?** The whole thesis assumes a
   proof-of-real signal at point-of-discovery converts admiration into purchase. Untested.
4. **Team risk (Jul 16 vote):** venture ambition vs. teammates optimizing for the pitch. The direction
   above needs to survive a team that may want a lighter demo.

---

## 5. Validation gaps — what we do NOT yet have (highest priority)

Ranked by how badly the venture thesis needs them:

1. **A committed, high-frequency local buyer.** Every buyer here was low-frequency. We have never heard
   the person our thesis assumes exists. **#1 gap.**
2. **A full-time, livelihood-dependent seller.** File 05 is a seasonal side-hustler ("don't invest
   much"). A maker whose rent depends on Etsy will answer marketing/price/relationship questions
   oppositely. **#2 gap.**
3. **A willingness-to-pay number** — buyer premium for "real," seller budget for tooling.
4. **"AI-made" in isolation** — is it a distinct fear or a synonym for "fake"?
5. **Neutral, non-leading re-ask** of the authenticity/anti-fake themes to de-bias magnitude.
