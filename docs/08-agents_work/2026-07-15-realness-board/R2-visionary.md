## The question on the table

Broad-adversary made the sharpest R1 argument: Trust-Graph is the only generatively-AI-resistant primitive because the signal is the *relationship*, not the pixel. Sora 3 ships this fall; the "unfakeable video" claim has a public expiration date; therefore Trust-Graph must be the spine and In-the-Making must be demoted to demo scene #1. Risk-modeler independently arrived at the same reframe from a different direction — the primitive is not "unfakeable video," it is **"identity-bound signed-at-source with named-seller accountability."**

My R1 tried to dissolve the spine debate with a category frame ("Human Provenance Network") — three rungs of one OS. Broad-adversary would (correctly) call that diplomatic cover: someone still has to pick slide 1, and slide 1 should be the durable move.

Here is what I got wrong in R1, what I hold, and what changes.

## What I got wrong: the category frame was underspecified

I named the category — "Human Provenance Network" — but I did not name the **primitive**. That let the frame float. Risk-modeler's reframe fills the gap: the primitive is **identity-bound reputation**, propagated through humans, staked on every artifact and every vouch. "Provenance" is what it produces; "identity-at-stake" is the atomic unit.

Once the primitive is named correctly, broad-adversary's Sora/Veo argument stops selecting between rungs. It selects for the primitive: any rung that encodes identity-binding survives generative video, because the durable signal is not "this pixel is real" but "this artifact is signed by a named human whose store dies if it's a fake." Trust-Graph encodes identity → identity binding. In-the-Making, correctly built, encodes identity → object binding. Both are reputation-backed. **Neither is Sora-fakeable, because Sora cannot fake the consequences of getting caught.**

That collapses the durability gap between the two rungs. Broad-adversary's Sora point is right about the *wrong* framing of In-the-Making ("unfakeable video") and wrong about the identity-bound framing risk-modeler surfaced. The category frame absorbs the critique — but only if slide 1 leads with the primitive, not the wedge.

## What I hold: demo the atom, spine the primitive

Broad-adversary's structural claim — Trust-Graph as spine, In-the-Making as scene — has one weakness the prosecutor did not price. In a pitch judged by product operators, **sensory encounter carries the room**. Trust-Graph as slide 1 gives Dina Murphy a diagram. In-the-Making as slide 1 gives her a moment: a Gen Z buyer opens Etsy on a bus in Brooklyn and watches Priya Kapoor's hands cut the leather for her wallet, timestamped, signed, staked on Priya's store. The panel does not decide between rungs. They decide whether the primitive is real to them. Sensory encounter is how a primitive becomes real to a product panel in 4 minutes.

Trust-Graph is the more durable *product* but the weaker *pitch opening*, because its evidence base (vouches accumulate across time) does not play in a demo window. In-the-Making is the weaker product-in-isolation but the stronger pitch opening for the identity-binding primitive.

The horizon-3 truth: **both rungs are load-bearing; neither is optional; the spine is the primitive that runs through both.**

## What changes: three updates to the R1 conditions

1. **Rename slide 1 to name the primitive, not the wedge.** Working title: *"Identity-Bound Commerce: The Human Provenance Network."* Subhead names the mechanism ("every object signed by a named human whose reputation dies if it's a fake"). This is the answer to the Sora question before it is asked. The demo of In-the-Making follows as the first visible expression.

2. **Trust-Graph moves onto the same slide as In-the-Making, not slide 12.** In the pitch structure I proposed in R1, In-the-Making was the demo and Trust-Graph was the 12-24mo roadmap. That reads to a panel as "wedge now, hand-wave later." Fix: show both rungs in the same frame as two expressions of one primitive — Priya's clip is signed by Priya's identity node, which is also vouched-for by three other verified makers in her graph. One slide, both rungs, one primitive. Broad-adversary's durability concern gets answered in the demo itself, not in the roadmap slide.

3. **If Day-4 maker-consent probe fails, the primitive holds and the demo pivots — the category does not.** Broad-adversary's kill-condition is <60% unassisted maker consent to per-order capture. If it fires, In-the-Making as demo dies, but "identity-bound Human Provenance Network" as category survives — pivot the demo to a hand-seeded Trust-Graph of 40 real makers vouching for each other, with capture-atoms as the vouch payload. The category frame is a hedge across broad-adversary's kill-condition, which is what a good horizon-3 frame is *for*.

## Where the 10-year primitive lives and which spine gets us there fastest

The 10-year primitive is **reputation-staked human identity, portable across objects and platforms.** In 2035 the shopper's question is not "is this real?" — it is "who vouches for who made this, and what happens to them if they're wrong?" That is a Trust-Graph-shaped world. Broad-adversary is right about the terminal state.

But the fastest path to that world runs through the rung a shopper can *feel*, not the rung an operator can diagram. Identity-binding becomes intuitive when you watch Priya's hands. It stays intuitive when Priya vouches for Marcus. It scales when Marcus's vouch surfaces Priya's clip in a stranger's feed. Lead with the felt rung; the diagrammatic rung follows because the primitive is already legible.

Spine order for horizon-3, in one line: **identity-binding primitive → In-the-Making (felt) → Trust-Graph (durable) → Follow-the-Hands (frequent).** Ship in that order. Pitch in that order. The category name is the spine; the rungs are its expressions.
