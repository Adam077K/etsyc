# Interview Guide v2 — Classmates / How We Shop Online Now

**Day 2 · Listening · "Each other"**
*(v1 archived — it pre-loaded the "real vs fake" frame and would have produced performed answers.)*

---

## What we're actually trying to find out

> **Does anyone actually care whether there's a person or a factory on the other end — or do they just want it fast and cheap?**

Not "do you value small businesses." Everyone says yes to that. It costs nothing to say.
We want to know whether it **shows up in behavior**, whether it's **worth money or time** to them, and whether it even **crosses their mind** in the first place.

---

## THE ONE RULE

**Do not say these words until the Dig section:**
> ~~small business~~ · ~~handmade~~ · ~~maker~~ · ~~artisan~~ · ~~real~~ · ~~authentic~~ · ~~human~~ · ~~support~~ · ~~AI~~ · ~~fake~~

Just talk about shopping. Normal shopping. Boring shopping.

**Then note the time-to-first-mention.** How many minutes in do *they* bring up a person, a shop owner, a small business — unprompted?
- Brings it up in the first 2 minutes → it's genuinely salient
- Only after you raise it → it's a value they hold but don't act on
- Never brings it up, even after you raise it → the whole premise is weaker than Etsy thinks

**That number is the finding.** Write it down for every person.

---

## OPEN — 2 min
*Warm. You know each other. Be a person, not a clipboard.*

> "How's the week going — has any of this actually been fun?"

> "Random one: how much stuff do you buy online, honestly? Like, is it a Sunday-night-doom-scroll thing, or are you a hunt-it-down-and-buy-it person?"

> **"What's the last thing you bought? Anything. The more boring the better."**
>
> *"Actually — can you pull it up? I want to see the real thing, not the version you remember."*

---

## EXPLORE — 6 min
*Just walk through their real shopping life. Genuinely curious. No agenda visible.*

> **1. "Walk me through that one. Where were you when you first saw it?"**
> *(feed? search? a friend sent it? an ad?)*

> **2. "How long from seeing it to buying it?"**
> *(seconds vs days — this is the impulse/deliberation axis, and it's the cleanest split you'll get)*

> **3. "What made you pick that one over the others? There's always three that look the same."**
> *(let them tell you their decision rule. Price? Reviews? Shipping? The photos? Do NOT offer options.)*

> **4. "Is there anything you've bought recently where you took your time? Where you actually thought about it?"**
> *(this is the fork. Everyone has both kinds of purchases. Get one of each.)*
> → then: **"What was different about that one?"**

> **5. "What's your… default? Like if you need something, where do your thumbs go?"**
> *(Amazon / TikTok Shop / Shein / Depop / Instagram / Google — the honest answer, including the ones they're a little embarrassed by)*

> **6. "Anything you've bought that you low-key regret? Or that showed up and was just… not it?"**
> *(warm, laughing — this is where the dropship/knockoff experience surfaces on its own, with no leading)*

**⏱️ Note the moment — if ever — they mention a person, a seller, a small shop, a creator.**

---

## DIG — 6 min
*Now you're allowed to open the door. But open it — don't push them through it.*

> **7. "Do you ever know who you're actually buying from?"**
> *(open, neutral, almost throwaway. Let them define it. Wait through the pause.)*

> **8. "Has there been a time you bought something specifically *because* of who was selling it?"**
> *(if yes → "what did they do that made the difference?" — this is the mechanism, get every detail)*
> *(if no → "has it ever even come up? Would you notice?" — an honest no here is gold. Don't rescue it.)*

> **9. THE TRADE-OFF — the core question of the whole interview.**
> **"Same exact product. One's from someone making it in their apartment — twelve bucks more, takes two weeks. The other's from a factory, cheap, here Thursday. Which one do you actually click?"**
>
> Then, no matter what they say: **"Okay — and be honest, what did you *actually* do the last time that was real?"**
>
> *(the gap between those two answers is the entire research project)*

> **10. "What would have to change for you to go the other way?"**
> *(price? proof? a photo? knowing their name? this is where the product idea lives — shut up and let them design it)*

> **11. Ladder the why — twice, gently:**
> "Why does that matter to you?" → *(they answer)* → "Hm. Why does *that* matter?"
> *Stop when they surprise themselves.*

---

## CLOSE — 2 min

> **12. "If you could change one thing about buying stuff online — anything, no rules — what would it be?"**

> **13. "Who's the most interesting person you know who *sells* something? Someone with an actual shop or a side thing — someone outside this room I could talk to?"**
> *(the referral. It's the only thing today that compounds — it's how you're talking to a real seller tomorrow while everyone else is still interviewing classmates.)*

> **14. "What should I have asked you that I didn't?"**

> "Thanks — this was genuinely useful."

---

## How to be, not just what to ask

- **Be curious, not clinical.** If it feels like a survey, they'll give you survey answers.
- **Let them be shallow.** If they say "I just wanted it cheap and fast" — *do not save them* by adding "but do you also value…?" That answer is the truth. Take it.
- **Count to three** after they stop talking. The second thing they say is the true thing.
- **Never yes/no.** If it's answerable in a word, rephrase it.
- **"Last time," never "usually."** Habits are stories people tell about themselves. Events are data.
- **Write their words, not your summary.** Paraphrase is where the insight dies.

## What I'll be looking for across all of them

**Three numbers and one gap.**

1. **Time-to-first-mention.** Across everyone. If the median is "never," Etsy's premise is in trouble and that's a real finding worth pitching.
2. **The trade-off split.** How many say they'd pay $12 and wait two weeks — versus how many *did*, the last time it was real and not hypothetical.
3. **Category dependence.** I suspect this whole thing collapses on category: nobody cares who made their phone charger, everybody cares who made the gift. If that's true, the entire challenge is a **gifting** problem, not a marketplace problem. Watch for it.

**And the gap:** every distance between what they said in Q9 and what they did in Q9-follow-up. That gap is the product.
