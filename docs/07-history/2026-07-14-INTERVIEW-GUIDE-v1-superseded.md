# Interview Guide v1 — Classmates / Online Shopping

**Day 2 · Listening · "Each other"**
**Subject:** online shopping in general — *not* Etsy. Never say the word Etsy.
**Why:** if you name the brand, they perform an opinion about the brand. We want the raw behavior underneath it.

---

## The one move that matters

**Make them open their phone.** Order history, DMs, saved posts, the tab they didn't buy from.
People are unreliable narrators of their own habits and *excellent* narrators of a specific artifact in front of them. Every question below is designed to end in *"show me."*

If you get nothing else right today, get the phone out.

---

## OPEN — 2 min
*You already know each other. Skip rapport. Skip "how's your week." Go straight to an artifact — it signals this is a real conversation, not a class exercise.*

> **"Open your phone — last thing you bought online. Anything. Doesn't matter how boring."**
>
> *(let them scroll)*
>
> **"What is it? Say it out loud."**

Backup if they resist the phone:
> "What's the last thing that showed up at your door?"

---

## EXPLORE — 6 min
*Real events. Last time, not usually. Follow the actual sequence.*

> **1. "Take me back to the beginning. Where were you when you first saw it?"**
> *(chasing: the discovery surface — feed, search, friend, ad, DM)*

> **2. "Who did you buy it from?"**
> *(the real test: do they even know? Person or company? Wait. Let the silence sit. If they don't know — that's the finding.)*

> **3. "Walk me through the last thing you bought from a *person* instead of a company. What actually made you buy it?"**
> *(from the exemplar — keep it. "Actually" is the load-bearing word.)*

> **4. "When's the last time you almost bought something and backed out at the final second? What tipped you off?"**
> *(from the exemplar — the single best question on the sheet. Abandonment is where trust visibly breaks. Get them to pull up that tab if they can.)*

> **5. "Show me a shop or a profile you looked at and thought *this is fake.* What did you see?"**
> *(don't ask what makes something fake — that gets you a theory. Make them point at one.)*

---

## DIG — 5 min
*Two tools: the say-do gap, and "why" three times.*

> **6. THE CONTRADICTION.** *(from the exemplar — deploy it as an accusation, warmly)*
> **"You probably say you care about supporting real makers. So when's the last time you bought the cheap knockoff anyway? What was going on that day?"**
>
> Then: **"What would've had to be true for you to pay the extra?"**

> **7. "After it arrived — did you ever think about the person who made it again?"**
> *(if no: "why not?" — this is the connection gap, live)*
> *(if yes: "what made you?" — this is the mechanism we're looking for)*

> **8. Ladder the why. Three times, no mercy:**
> "Why did that matter to you?" → "Why does *that* matter?" → "Why?"
> *Stop when they say something that surprises them.*

---

## CLOSE — 2 min
*They're a classmate, so "who else should I talk to?" is dead — same room, same people. Reach outside it.*

> **9. "If you had to coach an AI to seem like a real person selling online — what would you tell it to do?"**
> *(from the exemplar — the best question here, and it's not close. It inverts the problem. People can't list trust signals when asked directly, but they can absolutely tell you how to fake them. Every instruction they give you is a trust signal they hold but can't name. **Write these down verbatim.**)*
>
> Then, if they're on a roll: **"And what's the one thing you'd tell it it could never pull off?"**
> *(that's the unfakeable list — the brief's "time, skill, care, presence." Do not lead them to it.)*

> **10. "Who's the most *real* seller or maker you personally know — someone outside this room I could actually talk to?"**
> *(this is the referral. It's the only thing here that compounds — it's how you get to real makers by Day 3 while everyone else is still interviewing classmates.)*

> **11. "What's the one question you wish I'd asked?"**

---

## Rules (from the session)

- **Never lead.** No yes/no questions. If they can answer in one word, it was a bad question.
- **"Last time," never "usually."** Habits are fiction. Events are data.
- **Vague answer = you're not done.** "It just felt off" → *"What specifically felt off? Show me."*
- **Shut up.** Count to three after they stop talking. The second thing they say is the true thing.
- **Write verbatim.** Their words, not your paraphrase. Paraphrase is where insight goes to die.

## What I'm listening for across all of them

Not opinions. **Three things:**

1. **The signals they actually use** — the split-second tells that made them trust or bail. They will not be the signals they *say* they care about.
2. **The say-do gap** — every gap between stated value ("I support small makers") and logged behavior (bought the knockoff) is a product opportunity. The gap *is* the wedge.
3. **The moment connection did or didn't happen** — did the human on the other end ever become real to them? When? What did it?

> **Cross-reference:** the sponsor's brief asks how *time, skill, care, and presence* — the unfakeable things — could become things people *feel* when they shop. Question 9 gets there sideways, without ever putting the words in their mouth. See [`HLV-ETSY-CHALLENGE.md`](./HLV-ETSY-CHALLENGE.md).
